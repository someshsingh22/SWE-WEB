\section{Results and Discussions}
\label{sec:results}

We organize our results around three questions: \emph{which agents improve CWVs without causing regressions?} (\S\ref{sec:pareto-results}), \emph{does the agent framework or the LLM matter more?} (\S\ref{sec:ablations}), and \emph{what qualitative differences in planning and patching explain the performance gap?} (\S\ref{sec:qualitative}).

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reveals three performance tiers. A top tier (OpenCode+GPT-5.1\,Codex, Gemini\,2.5\,Pro, OpenCode+GPT-5, Claude~Code+Sonnet\,4.6) achieves Pareto improvement on 52--57\% of sites with degradation rates of 20--31\%. A middle tier (Claude~Code+Opus\,4.6/4.7, Gemini\,2.5\,Flash, Codex) clusters at 34--39\% Pareto with degradation rates approaching or exceeding the improvement rate. A bottom tier (Aider, Claude~Code+Sonnet\,4.5, OpenCode+GPT-4.1) fails almost entirely: 0--3.3\% Pareto improvement with 53--90\% of sites degraded. The gap between Health~$\Delta$ and Pareto Rate is informative: Claude~Code+Opus\,4.6 achieves the highest Health~$\Delta$ (+18.37) among all configurations but only 39.3\% Pareto rate, indicating that it produces large improvements on some metrics while causing regressions on others.

\subsection{Agent vs.\ LLM Ablations}
\label{sec:ablations}

Our experimental design enables two clean ablations that disentangle the contributions of the agent scaffold from the underlying language model. Figure~\ref{fig:radar-ablation} visualizes both ablations as radar plots over the six-dimensional Site Health Score space.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_radar_ablation.pdf}
    \caption{Site Health Score radar for the two ablation conditions, averaged across 300 websites (higher = healthier; 100 = at or below the CWV Good threshold, 50 = Needs Improvement boundary). \textbf{(a)}~Agent ablation with GPT-5 fixed: OpenCode's polygon fully envelops the baseline on all six axes, while Aider collapses on INP~Desktop, revealing the importance of the planning phase. \textbf{(b)}~LLM ablation with the agent scaffold held to plan-then-implement systems (OpenCode for GPT-5/5.1\,Codex/4.1; Claude~Code$^{\dagger}$ for Sonnet\,4.5 as an architecturally comparable proxy): GPT-5 and GPT-5.1\,Codex envelop the baseline whereas GPT-4.1 collapses on INP/CLS~Desktop and Sonnet\,4.5 collapses on CLS~Mobile/Desktop, showing that model capability governs side-effect avoidance.}
    \Description{A two-panel radar chart comparing Site Health Score dimensions across ablations. The left panel compares agent frameworks with GPT-5 fixed, where OpenCode dominates the other agents. The right panel compares language models under a matched scaffold, where GPT-5 and GPT-5.1 Codex outperform GPT-4.1 and Sonnet 4.5 on preserving overall metric health.}
    \label{fig:radar-ablation}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Agent ablation.}
Holding the LLM fixed at GPT-5, we compare OpenCode, Codex, and Aider (Figure~\ref{fig:radar-ablation}a).
OpenCode achieves 53.3\% Pareto rate, Codex 39.3\%, and Aider 3.3\%, a 16$\times$ gap between the best and worst agent frameworks using the identical model. Aider causes INP-Desktop regressions on 66.7\% of sites, consistent with its lack of a structured planning phase.

\paragraph{LLM ablation.}
To isolate the effect of the LLM, we compare three models under OpenCode and additionally include Claude~Code with Sonnet\,4.5 as an architecturally comparable proxy (Figure~\ref{fig:radar-ablation}b). GPT-5 and GPT-5.1\,Codex improve LCP while preserving all other metrics. GPT-4.1 collapses on INP-Desktop and CLS-Desktop despite matching GPT-5 on LCP (median normalized gain of +0.56 vs.\ +0.59; Appendix~\ref{app:per-metric}). A parallel pattern appears across Claude~Code models: Sonnet\,4.6 (51.7\% Pareto) avoids regressions that Sonnet\,4.5 (3.3\%) introduces on the same sites.

Taken together, the two ablations indicate that \emph{both} the agent scaffold and the LLM contribute to Pareto performance in separable ways: the scaffold determines whether the system can plan and structure multi-metric trade-offs, while the model determines whether it executes that plan without regressions.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis}
\label{sec:qualitative}

To understand the mechanisms behind the quantitative gaps, we analyzed the plans, patches, and agent logs produced for 120 representative websites spanning Poor LCP baselines, mixed-metric profiles, and sites where all metrics are already Good. We report quantitative patch statistics across all 300 sites and qualitative findings from targeted case studies.

\subsubsection{Precision Over Volume}
Table~\ref{tab:patch-stats} reports patch-level statistics. Patch volume alone does not predict Pareto success: OpenCode+GPT-5 and Claude~Code+Sonnet\,4.6 achieve comparable Pareto rates despite an order-of-magnitude difference in files modified and lines added. The scaffold shapes edit style --- OpenCode touches 5--9 files regardless of LLM, while Claude~Code ranges from 1 to 84 --- but within each scaffold, model capability determines whether edits cause regressions.

Plan verbosity does not predict quality either: Claude~Code produces the longest plans (1{,}533--2{,}337 words) yet spans 3.3--51.7\% Pareto, while the top configuration (OpenCode+GPT-5.1\,Codex) uses the shortest plans (474 words). What distinguishes top plans is \emph{specificity} --- GPT-5 references exact file paths and CSS selectors, whereas GPT-4.1 gives generic prescriptions --- and \emph{risk awareness}: on a site with Poor LCP but Good INP/CLS, GPT-5 explicitly plans to preserve healthy metrics, while GPT-4.1 makes no such mention.

\subsubsection{Failure Modes}
\label{sec:failure-modes}

Patch analysis reveals three recurring failure modes.

\paragraph{Async CSS without layout protection (CLS regression).}
Claude~Code (particularly Sonnet\,4.5 and Opus\,4.6) converts render-blocking stylesheets to async loading via the \texttt{media="print" onload="this.media='all'"} pattern, producing FOUC that generates CLS values of 0.86--1.08 on 16 of 30 sites (Appendix~\ref{app:patch-813-cc}). GPT-5 avoids this by keeping stylesheets render-blocking and adding a \texttt{<link rel="preload">} hint instead.

\paragraph{Resource over-prioritization (INP regression).}
Agents that preload multiple assets with \texttt{fetchpriority="high"} cause main-thread contention. GPT-4.1 preloads four thumbnails simultaneously, pushing INP~Desktop from 16\,ms to 752\,ms (Appendix~\ref{app:patch-404-gpt41}). GPT-5 preloads only the LCP image and defers all others. Claude~Code adds 116 \texttt{preload} directives per site on average, 47$\times$ more than GPT-5's 2.5.

\paragraph{Template application to already-Good sites.}
On sites where all metrics are already Good, every agent still applies optimizations. GPT-4.1 references non-existent files, generating 404 errors (Appendix~\ref{app:patch-952-gpt41}). GPT-5 acknowledges the Good baseline in its plan but still adds unnecessary rules, causing mild regressions. No agent produces an empty patch for an already-optimal site, suggesting all current systems lack a reliable ``do nothing'' judgment.

\subsubsection{Mechanism: Defer vs.\ Rewrite}

The single most effective LCP optimization is adding \texttt{defer} to render-blocking \texttt{<script>} tags. GPT-5 performs this surgically (${\sim}$9 defer attributes per site), while GPT-4.1 also defers scripts but additionally rewrites event handlers, restructures the DOM, and adds inline critical CSS, expanding patch surface area and introducing regressions. This pattern --- minimal targeted modification versus broad rewrite --- is the clearest qualitative predictor of Pareto success, suggesting GPT-5-class models have learned a form of \emph{surgical restraint}.
